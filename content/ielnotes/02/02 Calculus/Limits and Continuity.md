The *core concept* in [[Calculus (Main)]].

![[Limits Notation]]

Say, $f(x) = \frac{x-1}{x-1}$. On first glance, $f(x)$ could just be $1$, but really, $f(1) = \frac{1-1}{1-1}$ is *undefined*.

This means that there must be a *restraint*, a **limit**, which is written as $f(x) = 1, x \ne 1$. 

This would be graphed as ![[YAAAA]]

SO: $\lim_{ x \to 1 }$ would be equal to whatever number that y-axis is closest to.

![[calclimitsex2.png]]

This is the second example, basically the given is
$$
g(x) = \begin{cases}
x^2, & x \ne 2 \\
1, & x = 2
\end{cases}
$$
which basically means that $g(x)$ follows a parabolic curve—specifically, $x^2$—up until $x=2$, when it breaks that pattern and becomes $g(2)=1$. So when asked the value of $\lim_{ x \to 2 } g(x)$, it is simply asking *to what value is $y$ reaching as $x$ goes closer and closer to $x=2$?* The answer is four, which could be answered *visually*—by plotting the graph—or by solving the functions of $y$ while $x$ is going closer and closer to the limit $2$.
