okay gets, essentially, if it were $\int_{8}^{10}r(t)dt$, then  it would be the *second*, but since it is the $r'(t)$ (aka the *[[Derivative]]* of $r$ per $t$) it would be the *rate of change*, not the current rate of increase of subscribers. 

the way i understand it, it follows [[Velocity]] and [[Acceleration]] logic, where **derivatives** are the acceleration, the way your $r$ is changing, **integrals** are the *net change of $r$.

--- 

| Expression         | What It Represents                                         | Analogy                     |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------- |
| $r(t)$             | Current **rate** of subscriber gain (e.g., 100 subs/month) | **Velocity**                |
| $r'(t)$            | How **fast that rate is changing** (e.g., +3 subs/month²)  | **Acceleration**            |
| $\int_{}{}r(t)dt$  | **Net change in number of subscribers** over time          | Total **distance** traveled |
| $\int_{}{}r'(t)dt$ | **Net change in the rate** of subscriber gain              | Change in **velocity**      |
